RF Frequency Bands
How frequency determines your PCB material, trace width, via design, and shielding strategy
💡

Key rule: When your PCB trace length exceeds λ/20 (one-twentieth of the wavelength in the substrate), it must be treated as a transmission line — not just a wire. At 2.4 GHz in FR-4 (εr ≈ 4.4), λ/20 ≈ 14 mm.

Ku/K/Ka

V/W-Band
40 – 110 GHz

Wavelength in PCB substrate
λpcb = c / (f × √εeff)
c = 3 × 10⁸ m/s (speed of light)
f = signal frequency (Hz)
εeff = effective permittivity of the substrate (≈ 3.0–3.5 for microstrip on Rogers RO4003C)

Example: At 5 GHz on RO4003C (εeff ≈ 3.1): λpcb = 3×10⁸ / (5×10⁹ × √3.1) ≈ 34 mm → λ/20 ≈ 1.7 mm

⚠️

Copper roughness matters at mmWave: Standard electrodeposited (ED) copper has an Rz of 8–12 µm, which adds 15–30% conductor loss at 30 GHz. Use Reverse-Treated Foil (RTF) or Very Low Profile (VLP) copper for Ka-band and above. Some Rogers laminates specify HF/VLP foil for exactly this reason.

Transmission Line Structures
Microstrip, stripline, GCPW — choosing and designing each structure
MICROSTRIP

Microstrip

Signal trace on the top copper layer with a ground plane directly below, separated by dielectric. The most common RF structure — easy to probe and connect to SMA connectors.

Characteristic Impedance (Hammerstad simplified)
Z₀ ≈ (87 / √(εr + 1.41)) × ln(5.98·h / (0.8·W + t))
W = trace width, h = dielectric height, t = copper thickness, εr = substrate permittivity

⚠️ Open top surface makes microstrip susceptible to radiation losses above 10 GHz and sensitive to solder mask (Δε ≈ 0.5–0.7 increase).

STRIPLINE

Stripline

Signal trace buried between two ground planes. Fully shielded — no radiation, no coupling to other layers. Used for sensitive IF signals and filters where isolation is critical.

Characteristic Impedance (centered stripline)
Z₀ ≈ (60 / √εr) × ln(4b / (0.67π·(0.8W + t)))
b = total dielectric thickness between ground planes, W = trace width, t = copper thickness

✅ Best isolation. Slower wave propagation (lower εeff) means narrower trace width than microstrip for the same impedance.

GCPW

Grounded Coplanar Waveguide (GCPW)

Signal trace flanked by coplanar ground conductors on the same layer, plus a back ground plane. Offers wide bandwidth, easy transition to IC pads, and lower dispersion than microstrip at mmWave.

Effective permittivity (GCPW)
εeff = (εr + 1) / 2
Z₀ computed via conformal mapping — use a 2.5D EM solver (Sonnet, HFSS, AWR Microwave Office) for production accuracy. Gap width G typically equals trace width W.

🔑 Via stitching between coplanar grounds and back ground every λ/20 is mandatory to prevent parasitic slotline modes.

DIFFERENTIAL

Differential Coupled Lines

Two microstrip or stripline traces routed in parallel with controlled gap. Used for baluns, mixers, differential LNA inputs, and high-speed serial links at RF frequencies.

Odd-mode impedance (target: Zodd = Zdiff/2)
Zdiff = 2 × Zodd = 100 Ω (typical)
Coupling coefficient K = (Zeven - Zodd) / (Zeven + Zodd)
Keep S/W ratio ≥ 2 for weak coupling; ≤ 0.5 for tight coupling (>10 dB)

Matched routing length to within ±50 mil at GHz; ±5 mil for multi-GHz serial links.

RF PCB Stackup Design
Layer ordering strategies for RF + digital mixed boards and pure RF designs

Stackup determines how much control you have over RF trace impedance, signal isolation, and heat dissipation. For mixed RF/digital boards, the goal is a dedicated RF layer sandwiched between unbroken ground planes.

RF and digital sections should have a physical copper guard between them. Place RF circuitry in one quadrant of the board.

✅

Mixed-signal partition rule: Draw a physical line on your board separating the RF section from digital. No digital trace should cross under an RF trace. No power trace should pass through the RF ground plane. Use a single-point or bridge connection between RF and digital grounds to avoid ground loops.

RF PCB Layout Rules
Critical routing, placement, and copper pour rules for RF circuits
Parameter Rule / Value Rationale
Trace-to-trace clearance (RF) Minimum 3× trace width; ideally ≥ 5× for isolation > 20 dB Prevents capacitive coupling and unwanted resonances between adjacent RF lines
RF trace bends Use circular arcs or 45° miters; never 90° corners 90° corners cause discontinuities (reflection ≈ −35 dB equivalent at 10 GHz) and excess inductance
Decoupling cap placement Within 0.5–1 mm of device power pin; series inductor (ferrite bead) between RF and digital supply Minimises supply impedance at RF frequency; prevents digital noise from coupling to LNA/PA bias
Ground via spacing (beneath RF trace) ≤ λ/20 at highest operating frequency Prevents ground plane resonance and reduces return path impedance
Reference plane void under RF Zero voids under RF trace for the full signal path length Any void/cutout changes εeff locally, creating an impedance bump and reflection
Via stub suppression Back-drill or blind/buried vias for layers above 10 GHz A 1 mm via stub resonates near 30 GHz and causes deep notch in insertion loss
Solder mask on RF traces Open mask (no solder mask over RF traces) or specify εr of mask material LPI solder mask adds 0.5–1 to local εeff, shifting Z₀ by 3–8 Ω if uncorrected
Copper pour (flooding) Hatch or avoid pour within 3× trace width of RF traces; solid pour only where properly stitched to ground Unconnected or poorly stitched copper shapes act as parasitic patches and resonators
RF component keep-out Place LNA, PA, mixer before routing; minimum 5 mm from board edge Edge discontinuities and connector launch geometry must be accounted for from the start
Thermal relief on RF GND pads Avoid thermal relief on ground pads of RF components — use direct connection Thermal relief adds inductance to ground pad; at GHz frequencies this introduces series resonance

Via Fencing, Stitching & Shielding
Ground vias are the RF designer's most powerful layout tool — and the most abused
VIA FENCE

Via Fence (Guard Ring)

A row of ground vias on both sides of an RF trace, spaced ≤ λ/20 apart. Suppresses surface wave propagation and reduces radiation. Essential for GCPW and above 6 GHz microstrip.

Max via pitch
p ≤ λpcb / 20
Example at 10 GHz on RO4003C: p ≤ 1.6 mm
STITCHING

Ground Plane Stitching

Connects multiple ground planes together to maintain equipotential ground references across layers. Prevents low-frequency resonances in the ground structure. Distribute uniformly across board, especially around RF components.

Standard rule: one stitch via per 50–100 mm² of ground area on low-freq boards; every 5–10 mm² for mmWave designs.

CAVITY SHIELD

Metal Cavity / Can Shield

A soldered metal enclosure over the RF circuit provides 40–80 dB additional isolation. Required for LNA, VCO, and filter blocks in products that must pass FCC Part 15 or CE RED.

Design the shield can footprint into the schematic from day one. Ensure keep-out matches the can boundary. Add castellated vias at shield perimeter.

Aperture shielding effectiveness (SE)
SEaperture ≈ 20·log₁₀(λ / (2·L)) dB
L = longest aperture dimension (hole, slot, or gap in shield wall)
λ = wavelength at frequency of concern
Rule of thumb: any aperture (connector hole, ventilation slot) should be ≤ λ/20 for SE > 20 dB
A 10 mm slot reduces SE to ~20 dB at 3 GHz — a common and costly mistake

Impedance Matching Networks
L-match, π-match, T-match, and transmission-line matching on PCB
L-MATCH

L-Network (Narrowband)

The simplest matching network — one series element and one shunt element. Transforms from source impedance ZS to load ZL.

Q-factor (transformation ratio)
Q = √((Rhigh / Rlow) − 1)
Series element (Xs) = Q × Rlow
Shunt element (Xp) = Rhigh / Q
Higher Q → narrower bandwidth, greater transformation ratio
π & T MATCH

π / T Networks (Wideband)

Three-element networks that allow independent control of bandwidth (Q) and impedance ratio. Standard in PA output networks and filter designs.

π-match: two shunt elements + one series element. T-match: two series + one shunt. Use π-match when Zsource and Zload are both high; T-match for both low.

Target Q in PA design: 5–10 for class-A/B, 2–4 for wideband.

Quarter-wave transmission line transformer
Z0,TL = √(ZS × ZL)
A λ/4 section of a PCB transmission line with characteristic impedance Z0,TL transforms ZL → ZS.
Narrowband (BW ≈ 20% around centre frequency) but very low-loss on RF substrates. No components needed — only PCB geometry.
Example: Transform 25 Ω PA output to 50 Ω system → Z0,TL = √(25 × 50) = 35.4 Ω
🔧

Smith Chart workflow: Always plot the unmatched impedance on a Smith Chart (ADS, Keysight, or the free SimSmith tool) before designing the matching network. Move toward the centre (50 Ω) using LC arcs. A single rotation clockwise on the constant-G circle is a shunt capacitor; counterclockwise is a shunt inductor. Real discrete components add parasitics — derate Q by 30–50% for SMD inductors below 0402 size at GHz frequencies.

Common RF PCB Mistakes
Errors that cause board spins — from experienced digital engineers entering RF
01
Designing RF in FR-4 above 2.4 GHz FR-4 loss tangent (~0.02) causes 2–4 dB/10 cm insertion loss at 5 GHz. A filter designed in FR-4 at S-band will fail spectral mask requirements. Always prototype RF subsystems on the target substrate.
02
Assuming all ground vias are equal A via has inductance: Lvia ≈ (h/5) × [ln(4h/d) + 1] nH, where h is via length and d is drill diameter. At 10 GHz, a 1.6 mm via adds ≈ 0.3 nH — enough to shift a matching network by several hundred MHz. Use back-drilled or laser-drilled microvias in RF signal paths.
03
Routing digital traces across the RF ground plane Every slot or cutout in the reference plane raises the effective impedance and creates radiation from the current detour. Even a 0.3 mm DRC-passing via-clearance gap under a 5 GHz microstrip trace will cause a 15–25% impedance bump and radiate.
04
Ignoring solder mask over RF traces Standard LPI solder mask (εr ≈ 3.5) over a 50 Ω microstrip trace shifts Z₀ to ~44–46 Ω — a −0.3 dB return loss error. Specify SM openings over all RF traces in your Gerber layers. Always validate final impedance with the mask in the stackup model.
05
Poor SMA / edge connector launch design The connector launch is the single biggest impedance discontinuity in most RF boards. The SMA pin pad must be matched to 50 Ω — this often requires a tapered pad or a launch section. Remove the ground plane under the SMA center pin. Model the connector footprint in 3D EM.
06
Placing oscillators and PLLs too close to LNAs VCO phase noise and harmonic injection is the leading cause of receiver desensitization in wireless designs. Minimum spacing: 20 mm + shielded section between LO/synthesizer and LNA input. Budget at least −80 dBm coupling limit between LO and LNA input.
07
Unmatched PCB antenna feed A PCB trace antenna (monopole, patch, IFA) has a feed-point impedance of 20–200 Ω — not 50 Ω. Connecting it directly to an IC RF port without a matching network typically causes 6–10 dB gain loss and poor frequency accuracy. Always measure with a VNA and tune with a π-match or short transmission-line stub.
08
No RF pre-layout simulation Routing RF by eye and hoping for 50 Ω is guesswork. Use at minimum a 2D cross-section impedance calculator; for production use a 2.5D planar EM solver (Sonnet Lite is free). EM simulation takes hours and saves board spins that cost weeks.
